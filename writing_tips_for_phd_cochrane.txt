MAIN PURPOSE / GOAL OF THE GUIDE:
To provide PhD students in economics and finance with practical, highly opinionated advice on how to structure, write, and present their research papers effectively. The author emphasizes that academic papers are essays, urging students to eliminate fluff, organize in a "newspaper" style that reveals the main contribution immediately, and prioritize clear economic identification over complex mathematics or excessive literature reviews .  LIST OF TIPS / RULES / RECOMMENDATIONS:Organization & StructureFigure out the one central and novel contribution of your paper and write it in one concrete paragraph.  Explanation: Readers are busy and skim; you must make it easy for them to grasp the basic result without searching for it .  Example: Fama and French (1992) clearly state their main finding about size and book-to-market equity right in the abstract.  Organize the paper in "triangular" or "newspaper" style.  Explanation: Put the punchline right up front and fill in the background details later, rather than writing a mystery novel that saves the result for Table 12 .  The abstract should be 100-150 words and state what you find, not what you look for.  Limit the Introduction to about three pages and start immediately with your central contribution, avoiding philosophical throat-clearing .  Make the literature review a brief, separate section after the introduction .  Explanation: Readers cannot understand how your paper differs from others until they understand what your paper actually does . Set your paper off against the 2 or 3 closest current papers rather than writing a massive review.  Move robustness checks, general models, and extra literature discussions to an appendix or web appendix .  Writing Style & PhrasingKeep the paper as short as possible (maximum 40 pages) and do not repeat yourself.  Use the active voice and root out passive sentences.  Example: Write "I assume that $\tau=3$" instead of "it is assumed that $\tau=3$".  Use present tense and keep it consistent.  Use simple, short words instead of technical jargon or fancy words ("use" instead of "utilize").  Provide self-contained captions for tables and figures so a skimming reader understands them without searching the text.  Report numbers using two to three significant digits, not whatever the program spits out (e.g., 4.6 instead of 4.56783) .  Empirical WorkDescribe your identification strategy clearly.  Explanation: Explain the economic mechanism that caused dispersion in your variables, what constitutes the error term, and why the instruments are correlated with the right-hand variable but not the error .  Provide the stylized facts in the data that drive your result, not just estimates and p-values.  Explain the economic significance and magnitude of your results, not just their statistical significance .  Seminar PresentationsGet to the point immediately without a literature review, motivation, or results preview.  Example: Gene Fama starting seminars with "Look at table 1".  Do not put a bullet point for every word you intend to say on a slide; use slides only for equations, tables, graphs, and the most vital points .  Listen to questions all the way to the end, then count to three before answering.  KEY EXAMPLES OR CASE STUDIES MENTIONED:Fama and French 1992: Used as an example of a perfectly concrete abstract that states the exact variables and results.  Fama and French 1996 ("Multifactor explanations"): Cited as an excellent example of presenting empirical work by showing the stylized facts in the data (expected returns matching betas) rather than just relying on a formal statistical test (the GRS test, which failed) .  "Bond risk premia" (Cochrane and Piazzesi): Mentioned as an example of a "web-appendix gone wild" to store robust checks outside the main paper.  WARNINGS / COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID:Do not start the introduction with broad philosophy (e.g., "Financial economists have long wondered...") or extensive public policy motivation.  Do not write a "roadmap" paragraph at the end of the introduction (e.g., "Section 2 sets out the model...").  There should be nothing before the main result that a reader does not need to know in order to understand the main result. Do not include warmup exercises, preliminary estimates, or a travelogue of your failures .  Do not use footnotes for parenthetical comments; if it is important, put it in the text, and if not, delete it .  Do not use double adjectives (e.g., "very novel" or "striking results") .  Never use the words "illustrative test" or do empirical work you do not actually believe in.  Do not include all possible determinants as controls just to increase $R^2$ (e.g., including "industry" in a regression of wages on education, which obscures the mechanism) .  Do not spend the first 30 minutes of a seminar on literature reviews and previews, rushing the main results in the last 10 minutes .  CONCLUSIONS OR FINAL ADVICE FROM THE AUTHOR(S):
Cochrane concludes by reminding readers that economists are primarily writers, not just scientists who "write up" research. He notes that economics and finance papers are essentially essays, and good economists spend at least 50% (and up to 80%) of their project time strictly on writing. He advises students to pay close attention to the writing style of authors they admire and recommends specific books for further improvement, namely William Zinsser's On Writing Well and D. McCloskey's Rhetoric of Economics .  DIRECT QUOTATIONS:"Organize the paper in 'triangular' or 'newspaper' style, not in 'joke' or 'novel' style." (p. 2) 
"Here's the rule: There should be nothing before the main result that a reader does not need to know in order to understand the main result." (p. 4) 
"What are the three most important things for empirical work? Identification, Identification, Identification. Describe your identification strategy clearly." (p. 9)   

ADDITIONAL NOTES:
This guide is highly opinionated, direct, and heavily tailored to PhD students in quantitative fields like finance and economics, making frequent references to regressions, asset pricing, and econometric identification. The author explicitly encourages students to break traditional, boring academic writing habits.
