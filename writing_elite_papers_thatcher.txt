Authors: Jason Bennett Thatcher   Year: [not found, inferred to be circa 2024/2025 based on cited examples]Title: Rules for Writing Elite Information Systems Papers: Field Notes for my PhD Students   Publication (if any): [not found / unpublished field notes]Type of document: Guide / Tips for students   MAIN PURPOSE / GOAL OF THE GUIDE:
To provide PhD students with practical, actionable heuristics for writing theoretically grounded, construct-centered empirical papers that make clear contributions, specifically geared toward surviving peer review at elite Information Systems (IS) journals like MISQ and ISR.  LIST OF TIPS / RULES / RECOMMENDATIONS:Preparation & OutliningRule 0 (The Roth Rule): Outline before you write.Explanation: An outline is a theory for the paper. You must map theory/constructs to how they are tested (hypotheses/models) before writing begins to ensure clarity and genre fit .  Framing the StoryRule 1: Open with bold claims or puzzles.Explanation: Anchor your contribution immediately in the first sentence. Avoid historical overviews or generic "throat-clearing." End the intro with a resonant statement that you will return to in the conclusion .  Rule 2: Use narrative hooks.Explanation: Introduce dramatic tension using compelling empirical puzzles, contradictions, or unresolved paradoxes to make readers care before they even see the theory .  Example: "Despite spending billions on AI, many firms fail to adopt it strategically. Why? We show that the answer lies in whether the CIO has a seat at the table.".  Rule 3: Lead with the contribution.Explanation: Apply the "newspaper rule." Every section and paragraph must start with the most important theoretical or empirical claim, not background filler .  Rule 4: One construct, one paragraph.Explanation: Each focal construct needs its own paragraph containing: (a) a one-sentence definition, (b) boundary clarification (what it is not), and (c) placement within the nomological network.  Rule 5: Thread the construct throughout the paper.Explanation: Use the exact same term, label, and framing for a construct from start to finish. Inconsistency erodes reader trust .  Building the Argument (Theory and Logic)Rule 6: Use parallel logic and phrasing.Explanation: Logic should follow a clear chain (A -> B -> C). Concepts should appear in the exact same order in theory, methods, and results to reduce cognitive friction for the reader .  Rule 7: Link back to mechanisms.Explanation: Don't just report correlations; explain why effects happen. Frame results as theoretical systems in motion .  Rule 8: Theorize, don't speculate in future research.Explanation: Turn limitations into boundary conditions. Use findings to infer specific theoretical mechanisms for future tests rather than writing vague wish lists .  Rule 9: Model the journal you want to publish in.Explanation: Reverse-engineer 3-5 exemplar papers from your target journal (e.g., MISQ, ISR). Align your paper's tone, paragraph flow, and structural cadence with that journal's specific genre norms .  Executing Methods and ResultsRule 10: Frame empirics as theory tests.Explanation: Report results as tests of mechanisms, not a narration of statistical procedures. Start paragraphs with the finding and its theoretical meaning, not "We ran a regression..." .  Rule 11: The Gordon Davis Rule (Figures and Tables tell the story).Explanation: Every important element of your story should be visible in self-contained tables and figures. Reviewers should be able to verify your logic by scanning visuals alone .  Rule 12: Show everything that matters.Explanation: Make verification effortless. Every robustness check, alternative specification, and citation should be complete and visually accessible, not buried .  Writing with Clarity (Style and Rhetoric)Rule 13: Compress without hollowing.Explanation: Write dense, purposeful prose. Eliminate redundancy and hedging, but keep the sharp theoretical mechanism, effect size, and implication in the sentence .  Rule 14: Avoid stylistic clutter (Pinker's Law / Hemingway Corollary).Explanation: Eliminate hedges (might, may), nominalizations ("utilization" instead of "use"), shudder quotes, and metadiscourse ("This section will discuss..."). Use active verbs .  Surviving Review and Practical ImpactRule 15: Write for reviewers.Explanation: Anticipate concerns. Use footnotes to justify non-standard choices. A paper that is easy to verify and transparent builds reviewer trust .  Rule 16: End with resonance, not weakness.Explanation: Finish by confidently stating what you've established. Mirror the introduction to close the loop, and do not end with apologies or generic calls for more research .  Rule 17: Make the paper easy to teach.Explanation: If a colleague can explain your model on a whiteboard in three minutes, it is structurally sound. Teachable models are more portable and highly cited .  Bonus Mentorship PrinciplesThe May Maxim: Write with rigor for scholars, but keep it accessible and intelligible to the educated public .  The Pascoe Principle: Do not wait for inspiration. Treat writing like a disciplined job: show up daily, revise, and produce .  The George Law: Do not waste your advisor's time. Only bring clean, prepared drafts and clear questions to meetings .  KEY EXAMPLES OR CASE STUDIES MENTIONED:Li et al. (2021): Used to demonstrate how to open with a bold claim regarding CIO presence and AI orientation, and as an exemplar of the Gordon Davis Rule for using clear, self-contained visual models and tables .  Thatcher et al. (2018): Praised for leading with the contribution in the abstract ("We define IT mindfulness...") and for visually displaying a full path table with direct/indirect effects clearly .  Gerow et al. (2014): Highlighted for flawlessly threading construct labels, theorizing boundary conditions rather than speculating, and perfectly mimicking the MISQ journal cadence .  Chau et al. (2020): Used as the ultimate template for a centralized "Robustness Summary" table that packs outlier screening, multicollinearity, and endogeneity checks into one verifiable place .  WARNINGS / COMMON MISTAKES TO AVOID:Avoid historical "throat-clearing" in the intro (e.g., "The IS field has long been interested in...") .  Do not let "label drift" or "nickname drift" happen. Switching terms (e.g., from "IT mindfulness" to "tech vigilance") fragments your logic and frustrates reviewers .  Avoid writing a "black box" paper that reports inputs and outputs but fails to explain the psychological or theoretical mechanisms connecting them.  Do not use generic wrap-ups like "future research should explore" or "this paper has limitations." Frame limitations as explicit boundary conditions .  Never narrate statistical procedures in the results section ("We ran a regression..."); always frame empirics as tests of theory .  Do not hide robustness checks or alternative tests in unreferenced appendices; reviewers read visually and distrust hidden data.  CONCLUSIONS OR FINAL ADVICE FROM THE AUTHOR(S):
Thatcher concludes by reminding PhD students that these rules are heuristics gathered from decades of experience, not absolute commandments. They are designed to help new scholars structure papers, survive peer review, and avoid common rookie mistakes. However, as scholars mature, they must learn when to flex, blend, or break these rules to find their own authentic scholarly voice. He urges students to write papers that are teachable, readable, and trustworthy, and eventually pass on their own rules to the next generation. . ADDITIONAL NOTES:
The guide provides highly actionable appendices, including a "Common Diagnostics Box" (Appendix E) that teaches students how to mechanically check their work (e.g., extracting topic sentences to ensure they form a logical ladder, performing a "Hemingway pass" to purge passive voice). It also provides a ready-to-use "Response-to-Reviewers Matrix" and standard ethics/transparency footnote templates .
